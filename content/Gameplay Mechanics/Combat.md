


## Initiative / Order of turns

Unlike regular Dnd, the order or turns is NOT decided on a roll, but instead determined directly by the combatants [[Stats#Base Stats|Agility]], following the simple rule of, the higher the agility, the sooner your turn comes.

In cases where two combatants have the same agility stat, the tie is broken via a coin flip, where both choose one side of the coin, and whichever side it lands on, determines who goes first.

Initiative can also be affected by special [[Stats#Ability Points|Abilities]] or situations in ways that override the standard Agility method for getting the turn order, such as:


| Condition               | Effect                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Surprised / Ambushed    | Makes everyone who was surprised or ambushed, go AFTER the people who are otherwise ambushing or surprising. Ambushes can be prevented with an Ability.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                  |
| Preemptive Strike       | Affects only the users who have the ability equipped, or otherwise present as a passive. It makes this user go before everyone else, regardless of their agility                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| Preemptive Surprise     | When both conditions apply, to the same user, meaning, they gain the benefit from surprising the enemy, and have gained the preemptive strike benefit, they will instead act TWICE in the first round of combat, the first being before everyone else, be it allied or enemy, and then their second action in that round is placed at the end of the ally initiative, but before the enemy / surprised group initiative. |
| Various Status Ailments | Rule of thumb, if it boosts or nerfs a character's Agility, then it will proc a reorder of the initiative. For more information, Refer to [[Status Effects]]                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             |


## Actions, Bonus Actions, Reactions?

Within combat you have a wide variety of things you can do, be it use a skill, attack, fuck around, read a book, eat an apple, just about anything you can think of... however to avoid spending 5 in-game hours in someone building a boat while others fight, such actions must be filled within 3 categories.

Keep in mind that all that you can read here, also applies to enemies.

If your specified trigger doesn't happen however, your action was wasted, no resources spent though.)                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     |

You may notice that reactions are not really an expendable thing, unlike dnd, this is because of the Counter, evasion, and reflection mechanics, instead of active having one reaction per round, these hidden stats checked and then rolled against by the DM to determine whether or not you:

- Strike back with a regular attack, in the case of counters
- Take no damage and instead reflect the attack back at your enemy
- Take no damage by nimbly moving out of the way

This mechanic was implemented to give some semblance of coherence for obviously skilled classes, where in dnd you'd be at the mercy of armours and spells to dodge out of an attack (mostly happening with tanks with insane AC), leaving classes like Rogue or Bard, who're lightly equipped and meant to be nimble, to be the ones dodging the least.

There are however a few high level skills that provide a Pseudo-Reaction of sorts, such as Daystar, which, if used as an action makes the next attack the user receives become an assured counter, regardless of the type of attack it was, nullifying the damage and dealing an obscene amount of damage, it is costly however, and only works on the first attack the user receives.


## Movement and Opportunity Attacks

First of all, opportunity attacks don't exist, cry about it. If you don't know what they are, basically it's attacking someone just as they are running away from you as a REACTION, but since we don't have expendable reactions there's none of that.

Movement is pretty fair and simple, you have a limited amount of ft. you can use to move during your turn, and you can move attack move if you want, so long as you stay within the amount of feet your character can move.

To see what you Movement speed is (how many ft. you can move) refer to [[Stats#Movement Speed|Stats]]

## Death, and TPK

Unlike DnD where to revive someone you need A crap ton of resources, can only do it within a short period of time, and costs high level spell slots... here it's simpler, but with a caveat.

Items such as a "Phoenix Feather", or spells like "Raise" are enough to bring someone right out of death's door, however, this must be done within battle, and because "Reviving" someone is simpler, Death saves are gone.

If you're knocked out in battle, or otherwise killed by horrifying means (mostly), the soul of the player lingers on until the conflict has been resolved, via either running away, or the defeat of one side, leaving space for allies, or even enemies to use their resources to try and revive their teammates. 

If however, you're killed in battle, and your party runs away without reviving you, or beats the enemy group without reviving you, that means it is a perma-death, and will have to create a new character to join the game.

Now in the off-chance a TPK happens, there will be such a thing as checkpoints per se, if the players wish so, as in each town there will be a church you can pray in, if you do this, consider it a checkpoint, if everyone in the party gets killed, you will be put right back there, consider it a rewind or sorts, as the player characters will not be aware of this happening, so on that note, remember praying frequently at churches.

The other option, in case players are really reluctant to use the checkpoint System, would be to be brought back from death... without any of their items (Key Mission items excepted), as they would respawn back at the last town they visited, with no actual story or mission progress lost. If they wish to recover their items, they would need to go back to the place they died at, and defeat whatever killed them in the first place... That is, if they are still there.
